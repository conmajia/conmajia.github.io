    False writting, corruption, LoRs are not as promised as the purpose.
    Jan. 15, 2020 · 3 min · Samuel O. Alfred, Saebyukban Phil

    Aren’t letters of recommendation an inherently unfair aspect of the college admission process?#

    Original Quora page

    Samuel O. Alfred, Lead Administrator, Computational Mechanics and Artificial Intelligence

    Your question is a tough one but I deeply share in your sentiments.

    What goes on behind the scene before a recommendation letter gets to the Admission committee is usually undetected by the admissions committee. Though a letter not written by a Professor can be detected, it is not usually the case.(If you are interviewed before a decision is made, closely study the questions you will be asked. They reveal alot).

    It takes a great level of perusal to know that a Professor didn’t write the letter. Sometimes, students write the letters and their Professors edit it for use. Ultimately, all these defeat the purpose.

    Another worrying aspect is when members of the Admission committee tend to grade applicants by the popularity or ranking of their schools. They tend to favour letters from such schools in that regard. If by a stroke of luck your supervisor is known to someone on the committee or the Professor checking your application package , the odds might be in your favour. There are many good researchers but not all are as popular as the Professor Stingleys (Economics, Columbia University) or the Professor Muellers (Physics,UCLA,Berkeley) of this world.

    Even though your Professor has agreed to write a letter for you, he might just open his desktop folder of recommendation letters and edit one on your behalf. I mean, that doesn’t serve the purpose ( I know Professors are really busy but still, you’re unique so you shouldn’t use the same letter as others!). A generic letter hurts a student’s chances. Graduate school admission committee have what they look for. For PhD applications, Professors are looking for best fits into their lab. That’s why you don’t compare you chances of success with someone else’s profile. You can only do that if you have on good authority that, for instance, GRE scores were used to filter applicants. However, most often that nought , you don’t get that information.

    These factors are worrisome about the use of recommendation letters. However, this practice has been on for long and I’m not sure it will fade away soon.

    Nevertheless, since applications are assessed on a holistic basis, you have little to worry about. A captivating statement of purpose backed up with a good GPA and test score can make things work out well for you.

    Saebyukban Phil

    I agree with you completely.

    The biggest problem obviously being corruption. In countries like S.Korea, LoRs are highly illegal due to the fact that they naturally bring about favoritism and corruption. So, no matter how talented or smart you are, the opportunity will likely go to the prof’s favorite big-mouth brown-nosers. I see too many unqualified people getting what they want through LoRs in today’s society. And speaking of corruption, LoRs give the profs unethical amount of power over students because it is such a subjective domain, which may lead to exploitation of these young people.

    How well do these profs know their students, anyway? A few meetings about your projects? A few small talks over coffee breaks? Maybe a deep talk over a dinner if you are very very keen. If you think about it, even the biggest suck-ups don’t really get to know the prof and vice-versa. So, how unnatural is it for them to judge someone over these few moments?

    In my opinion, our society have got to get rid of this subjectivity from admission and hiring.
